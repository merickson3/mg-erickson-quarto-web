                  Milk-derived microRNAs (miRNAs) have attracted increasing attention due to their involvement in regulation of biological processes. However, the degree to which these molecules are conserved in infant formulas remains unclear. Herein, a high-throughput small RNA sequencing was performed to reveal the profile of miRNAs from camel milk, buffalo milk, and commercial infant formulas. Compared with raw milk, infant formulas exhibited significant reductions in miRNA diversity and abundance. Camel and buffalo milk were enriched in development- and immune-related miRNAs, comprising let-7, miR-2887, miR-2904, and miR-1246, whereas formulas reserved limited subsets at lower levels. Functional enrichment analysis indicated that raw milk–derived miRNAs were involved in immune-associated pathways, signifying conserved functions in immune regulation across species. Although both camel and buffalo milk display immunological significance through miRNA-facilitated gene regulation, camel milk miRNAs seem to target a broader spectrum of pathways, including those involved in cellular interactions and viral defense. Furthermore, protein–protein interaction analysis for the highly expressed miRNA targets identified central regulatory hubs such as HLA, LILRB1, LIN28B, and TUT4, involved in RNA processing, immunity, and stem cell maintenance. This study could offer a molecular foundation to support the expansion of next-generation infant formulas that more effectively retain functional RNA components of raw milk.

            <ce:abstract id="ceab10" view="all" class="author"><ce:abstract-sec id="ceabs10" view="all">
                  <ce:simple-para id="spara90" view="all">Milk-derived microRNAs (miRNAs) have attracted increasing attention due to their involvement in regulation of biological processes. However, the degree to which these molecules are conserved in infant formulas remains unclear. Herein, a high-throughput small RNA sequencing was performed to reveal the profile of miRNAs from camel milk, buffalo milk, and commercial infant formulas. Compared with raw milk, infant formulas exhibited significant reductions in miRNA diversity and abundance. Camel and buffalo milk were enriched in development- and immune-related miRNAs, comprising <ce:italic>let-7</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>miR-2887</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>miR-2904</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>miR-1246</ce:italic>, whereas formulas reserved limited subsets at lower levels. Functional enrichment analysis indicated that raw milk–derived miRNAs were involved in immune-associated pathways, signifying conserved functions in immune regulation across species. Although both camel and buffalo milk display immunological significance through miRNA-facilitated gene regulation, camel milk miRNAs seem to target a broader spectrum of pathways, including those involved in cellular interactions and viral defense. Furthermore, protein–protein interaction analysis for the highly expressed miRNA targets identified central regulatory hubs such as <ce:italic>HLA</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>LILRB1</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>LIN28B</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>TUT4</ce:italic>, involved in RNA processing, immunity, and stem cell maintenance. This study could offer a molecular foundation to support the expansion of next-generation infant formulas that more effectively retain functional RNA components of raw milk.</ce:simple-para>

               <ce:section id="cesec10" view="all">
                  <ce:section-title id="cestitle30">INTRODUCTION</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:para id="para10" view="all">In addition to its essential role in providing nutrition, milk is considered a functional bioactive fluid, containing higher levels of regulatory molecules that have the ability to influence newborn development (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref60" refid="bib17">Gregg et al., 2022</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref70" refid="bib44">Szyller et al., 2024</ce:cross-ref>). Among these molecules, microRNAs (<ce:bold>miRNAs</ce:bold>), small noncoding RNA involved in post-transcriptional genes regulation, have attracted increasing attention due to their functional role in metabolic programming, immune modulation, and cellular differentiation (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref80" refid="bib43">Słyk-Gulewska et al., 2023</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref90" refid="bib53">Xue et al., 2023</ce:cross-ref>). Allowing them to resist enzymatic degradation and potentially employ systemic functions in the targeted tissues, miRNAs are naturally encapsulated in the milk-derived extracellular vesicles (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref100" refid="bib4">Bai et al., 2025</ce:cross-ref>). Human milk has been broadly investigated for its miRNA profile (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref110" refid="bib52">Xi et al., 2016</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref120" refid="bib31">Liao et al., 2017</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref130" refid="bib8">Carrillo-Lozano et al., 2020</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref140" refid="bib24">Kim and Yi, 2020</ce:cross-ref>); however, the profiles of miRNAs in other milk species, such as camel and buffalo milk, which are extensively consumed in countless areas and recognized for their exceptional therapeutic and nutritional aspects, remain unclear. Various miRNAs that are plentiful in milk could potentially affect numerous functions. For instance, <ce:italic>miR-21</ce:italic> has been reported to regulate gut maturation and immunity, <ce:italic>miR-29b</ce:italic> could affect neurogenesis and regulate epigenetics, <ce:italic>miR-182–5p</ce:italic> is involved in immunity and metabolism, and <ce:italic>miR-148</ce:italic> could affect epigenetics and metabolism. Moreover, the <ce:italic>let-7</ce:italic> group is signified by various subcategories in milk and is known to be involved in several processes, such as metabolism, neurogenesis, and immunity (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref150" refid="bib29">Leroux et al., 2021</ce:cross-ref>).</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="para20" view="all">Infant formulas are designed to closely mimic the nutritional profile of human milk (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref160" refid="bib32">Logeshwaran et al., 2020</ce:cross-ref>); however, they undergo intensive processing stages such as pasteurization, spray-drying, and storage, that may compromise the integrity of bioactive components such as miRNAs (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref170" refid="bib12">Colella, 2021</ce:cross-ref>). The biological equivalence of breast-fed contrasted with formula-fed infants remains a matter of ongoing debate (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref180" refid="bib7">Caroli et al., 2021</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref190" refid="bib42">Slater et al., 2024</ce:cross-ref>), mainly regarding the immunological and epigenetic programming facilitated by milk-derived miRNAs. To bridge this knowledge gap, comparative studies are needed to evaluate the abundance, diversity, and functionality of miRNAs among natural milk and processed dairy products. To assess their functional potential and guide developments in infant formula design, it is also necessary to understand the extent to which miRNAs are conserved or lost in infant formulas. <ce:cross-ref id="cecref200" refid="bib28">Leiferman et al. (2019)</ce:cross-ref> reported that 221 miRNAs were identified in 3 human milk samples, among which 84 miRNAs were common in all samples. Conversely, miRNAs were not detected in infant formulas. Another study evaluated expression of 5 immunoregulatory miRNAs in human milk compared with infant formulas (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref210" refid="bib47">Vélez-Ixta et al., 2022</ce:cross-ref>). Relative expression levels of miRNAs were comparable among milk samples, and infant formulas displayed very low levels of these miRNAs. Similarly, infant formulas showed very low levels of <ce:italic>miR-148a</ce:italic> and <ce:italic>miR-125b</ce:italic> compared with human milk (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref220" refid="bib10">Chiba et al., 2022</ce:cross-ref>). In the present work, a high-throughput small RNA sequencing analysis was performed to reveal the profile of miRNAs from raw camel milk, buffalo milk, and 4 different commercial cow milk–based infant formula powders. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to provide a comprehensive analysis of miRNA profiles in raw milk (camel and buffalo) compared with different infant formulas.</ce:para>
               </ce:section>
               <ce:section id="cesec20" view="all">
                  <ce:section-title id="cestitle40">MATERIALS AND METHODS</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:section id="cesec30" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="cestitle50">Collection of Milk Samples and Infant Formulas</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="para30" view="all">Fresh raw camel and buffalo milk samples were collected from a local dairy supplier in Al Ain City, United Arab Emirates. The present study was accomplished within the normal handling animals' procedures approved by dairy farms. All valid established regulations for the use and care of animals were considered. The milk sample collection process followed routine methods and was out of the scope of Directive 2010/63/EU (article 1 5.f, “Practices not likely to cause pain, suffering, distress or lasting harm equivalent to, or higher than, that caused by the introduction of a needle in accordance with good veterinary practice”). Milk samples were collected under cooled conditions and immediately transferred to the laboratory and kept at −80°C until analysis. In addition, 4 different brands of cow milk–based infant formulas for ages 0 to 6 mo were procured from a local market. All chemicals and reagents were of high purity and analytical grade.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="cesec40" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="cestitle60">Extraction of RNA from Raw Milk and Infant Formulas</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="para40" view="all">To prepare the sample, 10 mL of each of the raw milk samples was added to 50-mL centrifuge tubes. For infant formulas, 5 g of the powder was mixed with 7 mL of 1× PBS and vortexed. Then, debris and fat were removed by centrifugation for 15 min at 3,000 × <ce:italic>g</ce:italic> and 4°C, and the fat layer floating at the top of the tube was carefully removed. The cell pellet was resuspended in 5 to 10 mL of RNase-free PBS, followed by a second centrifugation for 15 min at 3,000 × <ce:italic>g</ce:italic> and 4°C. The PBS was then removed, and the final cell pellet was retained. Cell lysis with Trizol was performed by adding 1 mL of Trizol to the pellet, followed by incubation in a water bath at 70°C for 5 min. The tube containing Trizol (1:5 ratio of Trizol volume) was thoroughly mixed with 200 µL of chloroform and centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 10 min at 4°C. The clear aqueous phase was carefully transferred to a new tube by using a 200-µL pipette, avoiding contamination. Afterward, 500 µL of isopropanol (1:1 ratio to aqueous phase) was added and gently mixed by inverting to precipitate RNA pellets by centrifugation at 12,000 rpm for 10 min at 4°C. To wash RNA pellets, the supernatant was cautiously discarded, and 600 µL of ethanol (70%) was added and gently inverted to wash the pellet, followed by centrifugation for 10 min at 12,000 rpm and 4°C. The supernatant was entirely discarded, and the pellets were air-dried for ∼5 min. Finally, 55 µL of RNase-free water was added to dissolve and elute the RNA.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="cesec50" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="cestitle70">Library Preparation and Quality Control</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="para50" view="all">For sample quality control, the concentration of RNA from each sample was determined by using Quant-iT RiboGreen RNA Assay Kit (Invitrogen, cat. no. R11490) and VICTOR Nivo Multimode Microplate Reader (PerkinElmer, part no. HH35000500). Furthermore, a 4200 TapeStation System (Agilent, part no. G2991BA) was applied to evaluate the quality. The RNA isolated from each sample was also used to construct sequencing libraries with the SMARTer smRNA-Seq Kit for Illumina, following the manufacturer's protocol. Briefly, input RNA is first polyadenylated to provide a priming sequence for an oligo-(dT) primer. cDNA synthesis was primed by the 3′ smRNA dT primer, which includes an adapter sequence at the 5′ end of each first-strand cDNA molecule. When the MMLV-derived PrimeScript reverse transcriptase (<ce:bold>RT</ce:bold>) reaches the 5′ end of each RNA template, it adds non-templated nucleotides, which are bound by the SMART smRNA, oligo-enhanced with locked nucleic acid (LNA) technology for greater sensitivity. In the template-switching step, PrimeScript RT uses the SMART smRNA Oligo as a template for the addition of a second adapter sequence to the 3′ end of each first-strand cDNA molecule. In the next step, full-length Illumina adapters (including index sequences for sample multiplexing) are added during PCR amplification. The forward PCR primer binds to the sequence added by the SMART smRNA Oligo, whereas the reverse PCR primer binds to the sequence added by the 3′ smRNA dT primer. The resulting library cDNA molecules include sequences required for clustering on an Illumina flow cell. The libraries were validated by checking the size, purity, and concentration on the Agilent Bioanalyzer. Furthermore, the libraries were pooled in equimolar amounts and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeqX (Illumina, Inc., San Diego, CA) instrument. In addition, image decomposition and quality value calculations were accomplished by using the modules of the Illumina pipeline.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="cesec60" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="cestitle80">Adapter Trimming</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="para60" view="all">To eliminate adapter sequences, the raw reads of small RNAs were pre-processed. Adapters in the raw reads were trimmed using the Cutadapt program (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref230" refid="bib33">Martin, 2011</ce:cross-ref>). The first 3 nucleotides of all reads were trimmed to remove extra bases, which were inserted during the SMART template-switching process. If a sequence was matched to more than the first 5 base pairs (bp) of the 3′ adapter sequence, it was considered as an adapter sequence and then trimmed from the read. Trimmed reads, whose lengths were longer than 18 bp, were selected for mapping reliability. Then, the remaining reads were classified into non-adapter reads, whose adapter sequences were not sequenced. In this analysis, trimmed and non-adapter reads were combined and regarded as processed reads for downstream analysis.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="cesec70" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="cestitle90">Clustering and Ribosomal RNA Filtering</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="para70" view="all">To minimize sequence redundancy for computational efficiency, processed reads were clustered by a sequence. A unique cluster consists of the reads whose sequences and lengths are same. To eliminate rRNA, reads were excluded that aligned to 45S pre-rRNA and mitochondrial rRNA. These filtering operations were performed only on <ce:italic>Bos taurus</ce:italic>.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="cesec80" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="cestitle100">Quantification of Mature miRNA Abundances</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="para80" view="all">Sequence alignment and detection of known and novel miRNAs were performed using miRDeep2 software algorithm (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref240" refid="bib16">Friedländer et al., 2012</ce:cross-ref>). rRNA-filtered or adapter-trimmed reads were aligned to the matured and precursor miRNAs of <ce:italic>B. taurus</ce:italic>, obtained from miRBase v22.1 (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref250" refid="bib26">Kozomara and Griffiths-Jones, 2014</ce:cross-ref>) using the miRDeep2 quantifier module. The miRDeep2 algorithm is based on the miRNA biogenesis model; it aligns reads to potential hairpin structures to check whether their mapping context is consistent with Dicer processing and assigns scores representing the probability that hairpins are true miRNA precursors. The reference genome of <ce:italic>B. taurus</ce:italic> (ARS-UCD1.2) was retrieved from RefSeq. The reference genome was indexed, and rRNA-filtered or adapter trimmed reads were mapped to it using Bowtie (1.1.2) according to <ce:cross-ref id="cecref260" refid="bib27">Langmead (2009)</ce:cross-ref>. Novel miRNAs were predicted from the mature, star, and loop sequences according to the RNAfold algorithm using miRDeep2. The RNAfold function uses the nearest-neighbor thermodynamic model to predict the minimum free-energy secondary structure of an RNA sequence. Then, uniquely clustered reads were sequentially aligned to the reference genome, miRBase v22.1, and noncoding RNA database RNAcentral release 14.0 (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref270" refid="bib37">RNAcentral Consortium, 2017</ce:cross-ref>) to identify known miRNAs and other types of RNA for classification. Processed reads from other species samples were aligned to the matured and precursor miRNAs of each species obtained from miRBase v22.1 (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref280" refid="bib38">Robinson et al., 2010</ce:cross-ref>) using the miRDeep2 quantifier module.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>

                     <ce:para id="para90" view="all">The highly expressed miRNAs from each sample were selected based on normalized read counts obtained from small RNA sequencing. The selected miRNAs were subjected to target gene prediction by using miRWalk, TargetScan, and miRTarBase, prioritizing experimentally validated interactions and conserved seed matches. Target genes of the selected miRNAs were analyzed for pathway enrichment using the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (<ce:bold>KEGG</ce:bold>) database. Furthermore, Gene Ontology (<ce:bold>GO</ce:bold>) enrichment analysis was performed by using the clusterProfiler package in R (R Foundation for Statistical Computing), applying a hypergeometric test to identify significantly overrepresented pathways. The analysis was accomplished by using the DAVID and clusterProfiler tools, applying Benjamini-Hochberg correction for multiple testing. All visualizations were created by using Matplotlib and Seaborn in Python. Gene network construction based on protein–protein interactions was performed using STRING software (Search Tool for the Retrieval of Interacting Genes, <ce:inter-ref id="interrefs10" xlink:href="https://string-db.org/" xlink:type="simple">https://string-db.org/</ce:inter-ref>).</ce:para>

                     <ce:section-title id="cestitle140">Raw Data and Trimming Data Statistics</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="para110" view="all">The total number of bases, reads, percentage of guanine and cytosine bases, Q20 (%), and Q30 (%) were calculated (Supplemental File S1, Table S2, see Notes). In camel milk, 69,775,367 reads were produced, and the total read bases were 3.6 gigabase pairs. The percentage of guanine and cytosine bases was 42.57%, and Q30 was 92.22%. Furthermore, the reads start at the first base after the 5′ sequencing adapter and in Illumina sequencing typically end after 51 bp (in case of sequencing 51 bp SE). As mature miRNAs are normally up to 25 bp in length, the reads will contain part of a 3′ adapter sequence that has to be removed. According to these criteria, the sequenced reads were categorized as trimmed reads, non-adapter reads, short reads, and low-quality reads. The adapter trimming process was performed to eliminate the adapter sequences that exist in the read using Cutadapt. If a read matches at least the first 5 bp of the 3′ adapter sequence, it is considered as an adapter sequence and then trimmed from the read. To be considered reliable for analysis, trimmed reads should be a minimum of 18 bp. Afterward, the 3′ end of each read was quality trimmed with −q 10 in Cutadapt. Further, trimmed or non-adapter reads with N base were regarded as low-quality and filtered. In the case of the SMARTer Small RNA library, an additional 3 bp base was generated during the reverse transcription process on the 5′ of read 1, so the process of trimming that base was included. The statistics of trimmed, non-adapter, and short FASTQ in the different samples are summarized in Supplemental File S1, Table S2.</ce:para>

                     <ce:para id="para130" view="all">The quantification step was divided into 3 procedures. This network indicates robust importance to post-transcriptional regulation, innate immune surveillance, and stress response, consistent with the immune-associated KEGG and GO profiles of buffalo milk.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="para240" view="all">The protein–protein interaction network in infant formula 1 is dominated by immune-associated proteins, comprising <ce:italic>HLA</ce:italic> class I molecules (<ce:italic>HLA-B</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>-C</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>-E</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>-F</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>-G</ce:italic>), <ce:italic>KIR</ce:italic> and <ce:italic>NKG2</ce:italic> family receptors, <ce:italic>LILR</ce:italic> family members, and <ce:italic>CD8B2</ce:italic>. This dense immune cluster reflects the KEGG enrichment in NK cell cytotoxicity, antigen presentation, and autoimmune pathways, signifying that infant formula 1 miRNAs may mimic some of the immunomodulatory functions of natural milk. In infant formula 2, the network features proteins such as <ce:italic>TRIM71</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>HMGA2</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>LIN28B</ce:italic>, which regulate RNA stability, chromatin structure, and stem cell pluripotency (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref610" refid="bib54">Yin et al., 2016</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref620" refid="bib36">Parisi et al., 2020</ce:cross-ref>). This network indicates a focus on epigenetic regulation, RNA interference, and developmental timing, aligning with GO enrichment analysis in gene silencing, RNA binding, and helicase activity. Infant formula 3, the network is positioned around apoptosis regulators and cell cycle, comprising <ce:italic>MYB</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>MCL1</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>CCND3</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>E2F1</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>CDC25A</ce:italic> (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref630" refid="bib14">Engeland, 2022</ce:cross-ref>). These proteins form firmly connected clusters intricate in G1/S transition, apoptotic regulation, and stem cell differentiation, supporting the role of infant formulas in growth regulation and tissues renewal. The network in infant formula 4 underscores oncogenic and signaling proteins, including <ce:italic>HRAS</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>NRAS</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>MYC</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>KRAS</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>LIN28A</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>IGF1R</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>HMGA2</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>ARID3B</ce:italic> (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref640" refid="bib49">Wang et al., 2016</ce:cross-ref>). These interactions indicate a strong focus on transcriptional regulation, Ras/MAPK signaling, and growth factor pathways, in line with KEGG enrichment in autophagy, signal transduction, and cancer-related pathways.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
               </ce:section>
               <ce:section id="cesec170" view="all">
                  <ce:section-title id="cestitle190">DISCUSSION</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:para id="para250" view="all">The present research provides an inclusive comparison of miRNA expression profiles in raw milk and commercial infant formulas, revealing both preserved and divergent outlines that reflect species-specific lactation biology and industrial processing effects. Several miRNAs, such as members of the <ce:italic>let-7</ce:italic> group, <ce:italic>miR-1246</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>miR-2887</ce:italic>, were consistently detected across all samples. These miRNAs are known to regulate cell proliferation, immune responses, and developmental pathways (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref650" refid="bib46">Tüfekci et al., 2014</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref660" refid="bib18">Harrandah et al., 2018</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref670" refid="bib30">Letafati et al., 2022</ce:cross-ref>), signifying conserved functional role in milk-derived miRNA profiles. The presence of these species in both natural and processed dairy products underlines their potential significance in newborn nutrition and development. Camel and buffalo milk exhibited distinct miRNA profiles; camel milk was enriched in <ce:italic>miR-2887</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>miR-2904</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>miR-1246</ce:italic>, which have been concerned in anti-inflammatory responses, metabolic regulation, and epithelial integrity. This aligns with the reputed therapeutic aspects of camel milk, particularly in metabolic and autoimmune disorders (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref680" refid="bib5">Behrouz et al., 2022</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref690" refid="bib2">Arain et al., 2023</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref700" refid="bib40">Sharma et al., 2025</ce:cross-ref>). The high expressions of <ce:italic>miR-1246</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>miR-29b</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>miR-141</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>miR-200c</ce:italic> in buffalo milk support its nutritional richness and potential roles in early immune programming (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref710" refid="bib25">Kittur et al., 2025</ce:cross-ref>). Moreover, the depleted miRNA contents in infant formulas may be attributed to the effects of processing steps, which may eliminate or break down miRNA molecules (<ce:cross-ref id="cecref720" refid="bib55">Zempleni et al., 2017</ce:cross-ref>; <ce:cross-ref id="cecref730" refid="bib9">Cemali et al., 2025</ce:cross-ref>). This decline raises questions about the biological equivalence of breast-fed versus formula-fed infants, mainly regarding milk miRNA-mediated epigenetic and immunological programming.</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="para260" view="all">The functional implications of the variation of miRNAs were further supported by KEGG and GO enrichment analysis. Camel and buffalo milk–derived miRNAs were enriched in pathways related to immune signaling, cellular interactions, and viral defense, aligning with the acknowledged immunomodulatory and therapeutic characteristics of these milk species. Conversely, infant formulas displayed reduced and more variable enrichment, with antiviral, immune cancer–linked and signaling pathways, indicating that bioactive regulatory potential of milk miRNAs is considerably compromised in the processed infant formulas. Moreover, protein–protein interaction network analyses of the top miRNA targets showed central regulatory hubs, comprising <ce:italic>LIN28B</ce:italic> and <ce:italic>TUT4</ce:italic>, which control stem cell maintenance and RNA processing. The incidence of these targets in milk samples but not in formulas further highlights the systemic regulatory potential of milk-derived miRNAs and their potential role in determining newborn development.</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="para270" view="all">Despite their detection in infant formulas, the limited abundance and diversity of miRNAs raise concerns about the ability of commercial infant formulas to imitate the full spectrum of biological functions offered by human milk. This is primarily relevant given that miRNAs are stable under severe conditions and given their encapsulation within milk extracellular vesicles, facilitating systemic delivery and efficient uptake by the targeted tissue. Furthermore, species-definite miRNAs profiles are emphasized, with camel milk typically enriched in <ce:italic>let-7</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>miR-2887</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>miR-2904</ce:italic>, and buffalo milk in <ce:italic>miR-2887</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>miR-1246</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>miR-2904</ce:italic>. These miRNAs are involved in epithelial-supportive, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective activities, signifying that raw milk may provide different developmental benefits. The clustering of raw milk in expression heatmaps, which are different from formulas, further supports the idea of biologically coherent miRNA composition in milk.</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="para280" view="all">Although this study offers an extensive analysis of miRNA profiles in raw camel and buffalo milk compared with different commercial infant formula powders, certain methodological limitations must be acknowledged. Precisely, extraction, characterization, or RNase protection assays specific to extracellular vesicles were not conducted. Hence, the presence, integrity, and encapsulation status of milk-derived miRNAs within extracellular vesicles remain unverified in our data set. This restricts our ability to conclusively attribute the observed stability and functionality of miRNAs to protection and delivery mediated by extracellular vesicles. In addition, due to limited resources, nanoparticle tracking analysis, transmission electron microscopy, or immunoblotting for standard extracellular vesicles markers were not determined. These analyses would have provided direct evidence of extracellular vesicles' presence and structural integrity, particularly in processed infant formulas, where vesicle disruption is likely. Despite these constraints, the results of the study provide valuable insight into the varying abundances and functional potentials of miRNAs across diverse milk types.</ce:para>
               </ce:section>
               <ce:section id="cesec180" view="all">
                  <ce:section-title id="cestitle200">CONCLUSIONS</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:para id="para290" view="all">The present study provides a comprehensive comparison of the miRNA profiles from raw milk and different brands of commercial infant formula powders, showing considerable depletion of functionally and nutritionally relevant miRNAs in infant formulas. The results revealed that the detected known mature miRNAs included 163, 273, 93, 90, 30, and 12 in camel milk, buffalo milk, formula 1, formula 2, formula 3, and formula 4, respectively. The enrichment of immune- and development-associated miRNAs in camel and buffalo milk, in addition to their contribution to conserved antiviral, immunological, and cell-interaction pathways, highlights their potential implication in early-life programming. Furthermore, network analysis showed crucial regulatory hubs, such as <ce:italic>HLA</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>LILRB1</ce:italic>, <ce:italic>LIN28B</ce:italic>, and <ce:italic>TUT4</ce:italic>, which may be involved in several processes, including RNA processing, immune regulation, and stem cell maintenance, yet are mainly absent in infant formulas. Overall, this study underscores an urgent necessity for next-generation infant formulas that preserve the functional potential and structural integrity of milk-derived miRNAs, thus narrowing the immunological and developmental gap between breast-fed and formula-fed infants. We acknowledge that the RNA extracted from raw milk somatic cells represents cellular miRNAs, whereas infant formula lacks viable cells and extracellular vesicles due to processing. Accordingly, the observed differences reflect variations in sample composition and processing, rather than direct depletion of equivalent miRNA sources. We recommend future work to include extracellular vesicle-specific isolation and characterization to further refine these comparisons.</ce:para>
               </ce:section>
