Abstract

CONTEXT

In Peru, silvopastoral systems have been included as a national measure to address deforestation and mitigate carbon emissions. Limited studies have assessed the sustainability of mixed livestock-crop systems using tools that address multiple Sustainable Development Goals (SDG).

OBJECTIVE

We assessed the sustainability of smallholder dairy farms in the Peruvian Amazon as affected by system type (silvopastoral or conventional) and herd size (medium or large) using the Tool for Agroecological Performance Evaluation (TAPE). Furthermore, we explored the linkages among TAPE indicators including the 10 Elements of Agroecology (EA), an overall evaluation scale (Characterization of Agroecological Transition; CAET), and 11 SDG-linked Core Criteria of Performance (CCP).

METHODS

Twenty-two farmers of the San Martin region were surveyed. Data were subjected to analysis of variance, Pearson correlations, and fitted to linear and quadratic functions.

RESULTS AND CONCLUSIONS

Silvopastoral systems showed a greater agroecological transition than conventional systems (69.0 vs. 60.2; CAET mean) regardless of herd size.
Results suggested that EA and CCP were essentially independent of each other if linearity was assumed. However, concave quadratic relationships were detected between the CAET and 4 CCP: Farm Income, Agricultural Net Income, Dietary Diversity, and Women's Empowerment. For these CCP, depressed values for farms with intermediate CAET (60 to 69) suggested that they neither reap the full benefits of agroecological practices found mainly in silvopastoral farms (CAET >70) nor the full benefits of conventional practices (CAET <60).

SIGNIFICANCE

The implementation of agroecological practices in smallholder systems may support positive economic and social sustainability outcomes.

1. Introduction

There is an ongoing debate on the role of livestock in the sustainable development of agriculture in Latin America. Livestock have been associated with deforestation (Wassenaar et al., 2007) and substantial greenhouse gas emissions (O'Mara, 2011), but they are critical to the livelihood of many poor smallholders (Herrero et al., 2009), and they provide ecosystem services for society at large scale (Sanderson et al., 2013; Alders et al., 2021). Sustainable intensification of agriculture can be viewed as the process through which productivity is enhanced without causing adverse environmental impacts or requiring the conversion of additional land (Pretty and Bharucha, 2014). Angelsen and Kaimowitz (2001) argued that the economic advantages derived from sustainable intensification might disincentivize deforestation or land acquisition for crop production. Similarly, Garcia et al. (2017) suggested that sustainable intensification of livestock in the Amazon could halt deforestation while creating positive social and environmental outcomes. Nevertheless, others have warned that the profitability of intensified systems could have the opposite effect, leading to higher conversion of forests into pasture lands (Kaimowitz and Angelsen, 2008). In Peru, cattle ranching is the second most important driver of deforestation after crop production (Vasquez Jara, 2019). Deforestation in the Peruvian Amazon has been substantial and continues to date. At the national level, the Peruvian Government has incorporated silvopastoral systems in its Nationally Determined Contribution to the Paris Agreement and has committed to increase land under silvopastoral practices by 119,000 ha by 2030 as a measure to reduce carbon emissions (Gobierno del Peru, 2020).
Silvopastoral systems purposely combine trees, pasture, and livestock. Thus, they are diversified and multifunctional. In Latin America, the implementation of silvopastoral systems has been limited due to technical, economic, and knowledge gaps (Arango et al., 2020). However, these systems have been purported to provide economic resilience to producers through diversification of income (milk, beef, fruits, timber) and environmental benefits to society, such as enhancing carbon sequestration and enriching biodiversity (Jose and Dollinger, 2019). Recently, Picasso and Pizarro (2024) indicated that benefits and tradeoffs of silvopastoral systems should be assessed in relation to the â€œstarting pointâ€ (primary forest versus degraded pastures). Noting considerable variability in methodology, specificity and assumption, Schader et al. (2014) concluded that a universal tool to assess agricultural and food systems sustainability does not exist. The hesitation of farmers to rely on sustainability assessment outcomes for decision-making has led authors to suggest that discussion, reflection, and learning should be considered as the primary outcomes of their implementation (De Olde et al., 2016). Thus, Wattiaux (2023) suggested using the Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) as a nationally vetted framework to identify context-specific farm-level metrics to explore synergies and tradeoffs while supporting agreed-upon global targets. In 2018, the Food and Agricultural Organization (FAO) of the United Nations released the Tool for Agroecological Performance Evaluation (TAPE) to assess agroecological transitions of agricultural production systems at the farm level, based on the 10 Elements of Agroecology (EA). TAPE is unique because it includes a set of Core Criteria of Performance (CCP) indicators that have been linked to 7 SDG concerned with a) human health and nutrition, b) economic performance, c) environmental impact, d) society and culture, and e) governance (FAO, 2019; Mottet et al., 2020).
In this study, we assessed the sustainability at the farm scale of smallholder farms with milk or cheese production as a primary economic activity in the San Martin region, which has one of the highest deforestation rates in the nation (515,364 ha between 2001 and 2023; MINAM, 2024). Data collected with TAPE was used to compare agroecological performance and test the hypothesis that farms classified as silvopastoral ranked higher than conventional farms on the TAPE's characterization of agroecological transition (CAET) scale regardless of herd size. A second objective was to explore the degree to which the EA were linked to each other and to the CAET. A third objective was to test the hypothesis that the CCP (i.e., SDG-linked indicators) were greater for farms classified as silvopastoral than conventional regardless of herd size. Finally, our fourth objective was to explore the data for evidence of linkages among agroecological practices and SDG-linked CCP indicators of TAPE. While relationships between single criteria are often studied, broader scopes of multicriteria analysis are still missing despite being promoted as methodological advances.

2. Materials and methods

2.1. Study design

The Institutional Review Board of Universidad Nacional Agraria La Molina (Lima, Peru) approved the procedures used in this study.

2.1.1. Study site and farm selection

Silvopastoral systems are not new in the Peruvian Amazon, where we have characterized livestock systems (Pizarro et al., 2020; Navarro et al., 2023). Our study site was CuÃ±umbuqui, which is the district specialized in the production of milk and cheese in the San Martin region. CuÃ±umbuqui has an area of 193,00 km2 and a population of 3301 inhabitants (INEI, 2017). The average altitude is 200 m above sea level with an average temperature, relative humidity, and monthly rainfall of 27.5 Â°C, 73.5 %, and 45 mm, respectively (SENAMHI, 2023). Eligible farms were selected from a Universidad Nacional Agraria La Molina database populated for a companion study using farmers' self-reported data (n = 71). Self-reported tree cover of grazing area was either 10 % or less (n = 52), 11â€“30 % (n = 17) or 31â€“50 % (n = 2). In this study, farms with 10 % or less tree cover of grazing area were classified as conventional (CON; n = 52), and those with 11 % or more were classified as silvopastoral (SPS, n = 19). In addition, farms were categorized by herd size, because of environmental concerns related to livestock. Using the INEI (2012) agricultural census categorization, herd size was classified as small (10 or less heads of cattle; n = 9), medium (MED; 11 to 49 heads of cattle; n = 19), or large (LRG; 50 or more heads of cattle, n = 43). Small herd size farms were not considered in our study because of limited sampling capacity. For this study, participants were identified with a purposive sampling method (Vogt and Johnson, 2016). Due to limited road access and time constraints for data collection, farmers from communities along the main paved road were invited in person to participate. Thus, farms in the study comprised 11 SPS farms (6 MED and 5 LRG) and 11 CON farms (5 MED and 6 LRG). These 22 farms were from 7 populated centers of CuÃ±umbuqui district: CuÃ±umbuqui (n = 1), Estero del Rio Mayo (n = 1), Difuntillo (n = 2), Saucal (n = 2), San Fernando (n = 9), San Francisco (n = 5), and San Francisco del Rio Mayo (n = 2).

2.1.2. TAPE and its implementation

The historical development of TAPE and its methodological details have been published by Mottet et al. (2020). Guidelines for application are available at FAO (2019) and training for enumerators is available at FAO (2021). Briefly, TAPE consists of 4 separate steps. Step 0 involves the description of the systems and their context through primary and secondary sources of information. This step identifies the background factors that are enabling the system to exist in its current state. Step 1 and Step 2 consist of a household (on-farm) survey data collection instrument. Step 1 focuses on the 10 EA: Diversity, Synergies, Efficiency, Recycling, Resilience, Culture & Food Traditions, Co-creation & Sharing of Knowledge, Human & Social Values, Circular & Solidarity Economy, and Responsible Governance. Each element comprises 3 to 4 indices, which are scored on a scale of 0 (undesirable) to 4 (desirable; Appendix Table A1). For each element, the sum of its indices is expressed as a percentage of the maximum score (either 12 or 16). Then, the CAET is calculated as the average of the percentage scores of the 10 EA. In addition, we followed El Mujtar et al. (2023), who proposed to calculate a Farm-CAET and a Community-CAET, each based on five of the 10 EA, with the former reflecting farm practices and the latter describing a set of broader factors that operate mainly at the community level (see Appendix Table A1). Step 2 of TAPE includes the following SDG-linked CCP: Secure Land Tenure (SDG 1, 2, 5), Productivity (SDG 2), Farm Income (SDG 1, 2, 10), Pesticide Exposure (SDG 3), Dietary Diversity (SDG 2), Women's Empowerment (SDG 2, 5), Youth Employment (SDG 8), Agricultural Biodiversity (SDG 2, 15), and Soil Health (SDG 1, 15). Added Value (SDG 10) was not considered in our study because participants reported no subsidies and no income from rent, which are the two items differentiating it from Farm Income. However, due to the nature of our study, two CCP were added: Income from Dairy and Agricultural Net Income. Each CCP, including the ones added, has its own method of assessment, the description of which can be found in FAO (2019) and Appendix Table A2. After the research team analyzes, interprets, and summarizes the data, Step 3 of TAPE is implemented as a community-based participatory process, allowing for validation of the results through meetings, workshops, reports, and/or peer-reviewed publications.
In this study, Step 0 data were from a variety of sources including literature (SÃ¡nchez-Huaripata and ArÃ©valo-ArÃ©valo, 2023), official records, and the expert knowledge of the research team who had worked with the community on related projects. Steps 1 and 2 data were collected between January and June 2022. Surveys were completed by hand using printed forms. Each survey was conducted with the head of household as the survey participants, who were men in most cases (n = 21). They answered all survey items, except those related to Women's Empowerment, which were expected to be addressed by women alone. Surveys were completed in one visit that lasted 2.5 to 3 h. However, Soil Health assessment of Step 2 was completed during a separate visit during which soil samples were collected for a companion study (Salazar et al., 2024). For Step 3, the research team conducted a half-day workshop in CuÃ±umbuqui in October 2023 to collect data related to the usefulness of the tool, and to stimulate a discussion on potential beneficial collective actions. These data are not part of this publication.

2.2. Calculation and statistical analysis

All the statistical procedures were conducted with R version 4.1.2 (R Core Team, 2021). For all analyses, an effect was reported as significant (P â‰¤ 0.05) or as a trend (0.05 < P â‰¤ 0.10). To test our hypothesis that SPS farms had greater EA and CAET scores (objective 1) and greater SDG-linked CCP (objective 3) than the CON farms regardless of herd size, we fitted the scores of each EA, the CAET score, the Farm-CAET score, the Community-CAET score, and the 11 CCP variables to the following model:
Yijk = Î¼ + Sysi + Sizj + (Sysi x Sizj) + eijk,
where Yijk is the response variable, Î¼ is the overall mean, Sysi is the fixed effect of the system, Sizj is the fixed effect of size, (Sysi x Sizj) is the system by size interaction, and eijk is the random residual error. Additionally, a paired t-test was performed between the Farm-CAET and the Community-CAET to test the hypothesis that the CAET might be improved with the adoption of agroecological practices that operate mainly at the farm level rather than at the community level. Visual inspection of the quantile-quantile (QQ) plots indicated skewness in the data distribution. However, we validated the adequateness of our statistics by generating residual plots and confirming the randomness of the residuals. Potential outliers were identified using scatter plots. After running the model with and without the potential outliers, the statistical inferences remained unchanged. Thus, all observations were included in this analysis.
Pearson correlations were used to test the hypothesis that the EA were related to each other and to the CAET (objective 2). For the 10 correlations between each EA and the CAET, an adjusted CAET (CAETa) was calculated without the score of the EA to which it was correlated to avoid autocorrelation. When a significance was detected, the relationship was studied further by correlating each of the indices (n = 3 or 4) of the EA to the CAET using Kendall rank correlation coefficient (Ï„) due to the ordinal nature of the variables. Significant Kendall correlations were used to identify the likely driver of the associations and thus a possible causal linkage between the EA and the CAET.
To meet objective 4, we used Pearson correlations between the EA and the CCP and regressed each CCP on the CAET score to test both linear and quadratic effects. When significance was detected, we analyzed the variables involved in calculating the indicators by computing descriptive statistics and relying on an analysis of the coefficient of variation (as a dispersion measure) to identify the possible drivers of the relationship.

3. Results

3.1. Description of local context

Research in the Amazon region has shown that grasses under SPS had a similar total forage accumulation, but greater nutritive value compared with open pastures (Do Nascimento et al., 2019; Gomes et al., 2022). In CuÃ±umbuqui, the land has a sloping topography with paddocks and property lines delimited with wire fences or trees used as live fences. SÃ¡nchez-Huaripata and ArÃ©valo-ArÃ©valo (2023) reported average farm size of 30 ha and 22 heads of dual-purpose cattle. Most of the cattle were crossbred (Gyr*Holstein and Gyr*Brown Swiss) used for both milk and beef production. Most farmers do the milking by hand once a day and milk production on average was 4.5 L/cow/day. Feeding was based mainly on cultivated pastures (Brachiaria brizantha), with limited management practices. Supplementation included cut-&-carry of forages such as elephant grass (Pennisetum purpureum). Milk production was used partially for household consumption but mostly for sale. The milk was collected daily by a local processor or by intermediary haulers who resell it to processing plants. Eventually, dairy products are sold in outdoor and street markets or in retail stores in Tarapoto, the largest city in the region (population 76,122; INEI, 2017). Based on the 22 observations of our study, we observed substantially more men than women living in the household (mean Â± SEM; 1.6 Â± 0.2 vs. 1.0 Â± 0.2). Most of the men (1.5 Â± 0.2) were responsible for the farm labor compared to women (0.3 Â± 0.1). Productive land (48.4 Â± 6.8 ha) was primarily pasture (30.3 Â± 4.2, ranging from 7 to 80 ha) grazed by cattle and some crops for consumption by humans or small livestock. 3.4. Linkages between agroecological practices and core criteria of performance

To satisfy our fourth objective, we explored all 110 possible correlations between the EA and the CCP of this study and fitted linear and quadratic relationships between the CAET and the 10 CCP exhibiting variability (Table 2). Only two correlations were significant (P < 0.05). The CCP Productivity (soles/ha/year) was correlated with both the EA Circular & Solidarity Economy (r = 0.53) and with the EA Responsible Governance (r = 0.47). Our analysis of the Kendall correlations in search of possible causal relationship suggested that the existence of a local food system was the index within Circular & Solidarity Economy that tended to be associated with Productivity (Ï„ = 0.29; P = 0.08) and farmer's participation in the governance of land and natural resources was the index within Responsible Governance that tended to be associated with Productivity (Ï„ = 0.30; P = 0.10).

4. Discussion

4.1. CAET score typology

To date, there are limited studies characterizing the agroecological transition of mixed crop-livestock systems using TAPE as a methodology, and this study marked the first time it was used to study smallholders specialized in milk production. Although comparing CAET scores between TAPE studies and countries should be done with precaution as context and data collection differ, the overall CAET reported here (64.6 Â± 2.0) was intermediate between the overall value reported by Lucantoni et al. (2023) for 233 small, diversified family farms in Mali (average of CAET 55.0) and the overall value reported by El Mujar et al. (2023) for 29 small, diversified farms in Nicaragua (median CAET 72.9). The ways by which livestock systems may contribute positively to an agroecological transition remain subject to debate (FAO, 2018; Sijpestijn et al., 2022). In Mali, the CAET of the smallholders managing livestock in mixed crop-livestock systems was similar to our average value and was much greater than the CAET of smallholders focused on monoculture cropping of cereals and legumes (CAET 60 vs 35, respectively; Lucantoni et al., 2023). However, the CAET scores for Nicaraguan crop farms integrating cattle production were lower than our average value and lower for farms mainly producing grains, vegetables, and fruits (CAET 66 vs. 80.9; El Mujtar et al., 2023).
Lucantoni et al. (2022) proposed a typology that refers to farms with CAET <40 as â€œconventionalâ€, those with 40 â‰¤ CAET <50 as â€œconventional with sustainability elementsâ€, those with 50 â‰¤ CAET <60 as â€œin initial transition to agroecologyâ€, those with 60 â‰¤ CAET <70 as â€œin transition to agroecologyâ€, and those with CAET â‰¥70 as â€œagroecologicalâ€. According to this typology the most common farm category in our study was â€œin transition to agroecologyâ€ (n = 8; 36 %) followed by â€œin initial transition to agroecologyâ€ (n = 7; 32 %), â€œagroecologicalâ€ (n = 6; 27 %), and â€œconventional with sustainability elementsâ€ (n = 1; 5 %). As a group, the CON farms (CAET 60.5 Â± 5.8) and the SPS farms (CAET 69.1 Â± 1.4) were respectively at the lower end and the higher end of the â€œin initial transition to agroecologyâ€ category. In this study the SPS farms, which were primarily (but not exclusively) the high-CAET score farms (Fig. 1, Fig. 3) had superior scores for Circular & Solidarity Economy (+ 16 pts), Resilience (+ 14 pts), Recycling (+13 pts), and Synergies (+10 pts) compared to CON farms (Table 1). Among these, Synergies and Circular & Solidarity Economy were also found to be positively associated with the CAET score (Fig. 2). In addition, our correlation analysis suggested also that greater Responsible Governance and greater Co-creation & Sharing of Knowledge were also important EA contributing positively to greater CAET score in this study. Furthermore, we found that 4 of the top 5 elements with the greatest scores (Culture & Food Traditions, Responsible Governance, Circular & Solidarity Economy, and Human & Social Values) were elements that operate mainly at the community level as defined in El Mujtar et al. (2023). Overall, farms in this study exhibited greater Community-CAET than Farm-CAET (69.0 Â± 6.3 vs. 60.7 Â± 5.9). In contrast El Mujtar et al. (2023) reported a lower Community-CAET compared to the Farm-CAET (median 69.2 vs. 75.0). Although the dichotomy between Farm-CAET vs. Community-CAET may seem appealing, the EA are not independent of each other as suggested by the correlations presented in Fig. 2. Thus, the validity of disaggregating the overall CAET in these 2 categories should be tested in future research as they might reflect farmers' perception of the various ways in which their farm is interconnected to a broader community (and vice versa).
There is a large variation in the SPS practices in different regions of the Peruvian Amazon (Pizarro et al., 2020; Fuentes et al., 2022). However, our findings suggested that enhancing farm-level management practices, such as Recycling (which scored the lowest at 36), as a possible priority to improve the agroecological performance of the farms of this study. Indices that explained the low score of this element included the limited recycling of farm residues and by-products, the lack of equipment for water harvesting or saving (e.g., water tanks), and the limited use and production of renewable energy (e.g., solar energy). The second lowest EA was Co-Creation & Sharing of Knowledge. In this case, the low score was due to farmers reporting a lack of platforms for the creation and transfer of knowledge and their low participation in farmers' organizations. Although these findings were obtained with a limited sample size and farms located near the main paved road, our results established a baseline assessment. Future research should be extended to a larger and more diversified farms to allow for the delineation of Amazon farm typologies based on the EA and the CCP as measured in TAPE.

4.2. Linkages between Agroecological practices and the SDG-linked CCP

The assessment metrics of TAPE align with both sustainable agriculture (i.e., the EA of Step 1) and global targets built in the SDG (i.e., SDG-linked CCP of Step 2). Whether a greater score for EA (viewed as the system's attributes) was associated with a greater CCP (viewed as the system's outcomes) was one of the motivations for conducting this study. For the livestock systems studied here, we found a limited number of correlations between the EA and the CCP. The nature of the positive correlations between Productivity and Circular & Solidarity Economy, and between Productivity and Responsible Governance should be studied further. These correlations may be unique to this study but suggested that either Productivity is important for creating an environment enabling the agroecological transition, or in reverse, that Responsible Governance and Circular & Solidarity Economy are important contributors to Productivity. Nevertheless, the paucity of correlations between the EA and the CCP suggested that they are essentially independent of each other (if linearity is to be assumed).
Our study also revealed concave quadratic relationships for 2 indicators of economic performance: Farm Income (soles/year) and Agricultural Net Income (soles/worker/year; Table 3 and Fig. 3). These results are in general agreement with Lucantoni et al. (2023) who reported that a series of indicators of economic performance exhibited lower values for farms with intermediate CAET than for farms with lower or higher CAET. The authors suggested that the depressed intermediate score reflected a period during which a farmer learned to master agroecological practices. However, this interpretation of results assumed that agroecological transition is a stepwise process: Farmers are testing and adopting (or rejecting) new management practices and technologies over time. Alternatively, for farmers who are averse to changes, the agroecological transition (i.e., CAET score of their operation) may reflect a long-term structural characteristic of a farm. Regardless of the time scale, our data suggested that farms with intermediate CAET (i.e., between 60 and 70) were not getting the full benefits from applying agroecological practices (as observed on farms with high CAET), nor the full economic benefits from greater reliance on external inputs (as observed for conventional farms with low CAET). Our analysis of the variables used to calculate Farm income (soles/year) suggested that the reason for which values were depressed for farms with intermediate CAET scores was because of a greater level of expenses as opposed to a lesser revenue from the sale of milk and cheese (data not shown). Similarly, an analysis of the variables used to calculate Agricultural Net Income (soles/worker/year) indicated that the depressed values for farms with intermediate CAET score was explained by the combined effects of a reduced agricultural net income (i.e., the numerator of this indicator) and greater number of workers on the farms (i.e., the denominator of this indicator). Namely, in this study, Agricultural Net Income averaged 33,217, 12,800 and 40,300 soles for low (CAET <60), intermediate (60 â‰¤ CAET â‰¤70), and high (CAET >70) CAET farms, whereas the number of workers in the same three CAET categories averaged 1.7, 2.1, and 1.8, respectively. These findings suggested that household composition might play a role in this indicator. Most of the participating farms in our survey relied almost exclusively on family labor and therefore available labor may vary over time with the age and stage of life of each household member. In this study, the average age of the head of household was 56.3 Â± 2.72 years and the structure of family labor is in Appendix Table A3. This table also showed that the number of youngsters who could contribute to youth employment on the farms was limited which was reflected also in the Human & Social Values index focused on Youth empowerment and emigration (data not shown). Most of the farmers have reported that youngsters think that agriculture is too hard and want to emigrate from the farm to the city. In contrast, Goris et al. (2021) have reported an active role of young agroecologists in valorizing traditional knowledge on agroecological practices in Minas Gerais, Brazil, as an alternative approach to agriculture, which opens the door to promote job opportunities.
Despite limited sample size, this study is the first, to the best of our knowledge, to suggest a quadratic relationship between the CAET and indicators of social performance, namely Women's Empowerment, and Dietary Diversity (Table 3 and Fig. 3). In some cases, the presence of the men while women were answering questions related to Women's Empowerment during data collection may have influenced our results. Nevertheless, a large-scale study in Ethiopia reported that both Women's Empowerment and Dietary Diversity were among the social indicators positively correlated with the CAET (Wordofa et al., 2024). Thus, large sample size studies in the future should explore both linear and non-linear relationships between agroecological performance and dimensions of social sustainability.

4.3. TAPE and other assessment tools

This study is the first to use TAPE to assess smallholder dairy systems in the region. Originally, one of the specific objectives of TAPE was to inform policymakers about the multi-dimensional performance of agroecology and its potential to contribute to the SDG (FAO, 2019). Logically a one-size-fits-all data collection tool allows for cross-national comparison and thus national-level decision making by policymakers. However, TAPE has also been used by non-governmental organizations to give a voice to the farming community. Under this lens, the tool should be reviewed for each application on a participatory basis with sufficient contextual details to facilitate the decision-making of the farmers (Lucantoni et al., 2022). Furthermore, over the last few years, TAPE has been increasingly used by inter- and transdisciplinary researchers focused on the sustainability of agriculture and food systems. Recent case studies revealed that TAPE, in its current form, is better-suited to the context of low- and-middle income- countries than high-income countries (Anthonioz, 2021; Handsotter, 2022). A case study carried out in France, for example, showed that Women's Empowerment scored low on the basis that women did not contribute to the farm's decision process, but the tool did not capture the substantial portion of women with no interest or involvement in farming activities (Anthonioz, 2021). Field observations indicated that some of these women had their own professions, and thus in a way were empowered beyond the confines of the farm. However, it is not clear the degree to which this situation results from an exclusion of the farm business as a form of disempowerment. Similarly, another case study carried out in Norway showed that TAPE needs adjustments to avoid incentivizing arguably sustainable but antiquated practices such as the use of animals for traction (Handsotter, 2022). Another limitation of the TAPE version that was used in this study is the lack of characterization of the livestock component of the livestock-crop mixed systems. This limitation, however, is being addressed in an upcoming version of TAPE. In this updated version, indexes used to calculate the scores for the EA include diversity of feed resources, ecosystem services provided by livestock, and recycling of animal by-products, among others.
Other tools have been used to perform sustainability assessments in the Peruvian Amazon. Using a multi-criteria method proposed by SarandÃ³n and Flores (2009) to assess the sustainability of smallholder livestock systems in Yurimaguas, Loreto (a region near San Martin), Van-Heurck et al. (2020) found that soil degradation, limited implementation of silvopastoral systems, and lack of farmers' organizations were some of the key concerns affecting the sustainability. Recently, Torres Jara De GarcÃ­a et al. (2023) found that low calving rates, undefined crossbreeds, limited animal water supply, and absence of reforestation practices contributed to low sustainability of smallholder livestock systems in Moyobamba in the San Martin region. The sustainability assessment tool used was an adaptation and modification of methodologies proposed by Altieri and Nicholls (2002), Nicholls et al. (2004), and Reis de Araujo et al. (2008).

5. Conclusions

Our results support the hypothesis that silvopastoral systems showed a more advanced agroecological transition than conventional systems, regardless of herd size. System type influenced 6 EA and 1 CCP (Productivity), whereas herd size influenced 3 EA and 1 CCP (Women's Empowerment). Although our results suggest that the EA are essentially independent of the CCP, we found that the CCP Productivity (soles/ha/year) was associated with the EA Circular & Solidarity Economy as well as with the EA Responsible Governance. In addition, our data suggested that Farm Income (soles/year) was driven more by lower levels of expenses than the revenue obtained from the milk or cheese sold. Additionally, a lower Agricultural Net Income (soles/worker/year) in the farms with intermediate CAET was associated with a greater number of farm workers and higher expenses than for farms with high or low CAET, while the lower Women's Empowerment in the farms with intermediate CAET was associated with a lower women's productive decision and leadership than for farms with a high or a low CAET. These results illustrate that agroecology may support both economic and social sustainability. Thus, future research should confirm and explore the nature of these relationships.
